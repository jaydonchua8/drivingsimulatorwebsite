Development Page

How we got here

This page documents the behind-the-scenes path from our first idea to the final scaled-down demo. We kept it honest: what we tried, what failed, and what we learned.

Timeline

  1. Early semester

    Big-scale brainstorming

    We started by exploring full simulator ideas. We looked into methods like linear actuators and other mechanisms that could physically move or resist a steering wheel for force feedback. The concepts were exciting, but we realized the complexity and cost were high for our time and resources.

    Decision: prove the core concept on a smaller scale first.

  2. Mid semester

    Scaling down to a single rotating motor

    We simplified: instead of multiple actuators, we aimed for one rotating motor output (clockwise and counterclockwise) that could represent steering direction. This let us iterate quickly and test the idea with basic parts.

  3. Later

    Servo/joystick attempts (and why they stalled)

    We attempted to control rotation using a joystick and experimented with different approaches. After multiple failed attempts, we recognized a key issue: reversing a DC motor isn’t just a software problem—bidirectional control typically needs an H-bridge.

    Lesson: matching the component (servo vs DC motor) to the control goal matters as much as code.

  4. Final weeks

    Final prototype: toy car on a rotating platform

    Our final demo mounts a toy model car on a rotating motor platform. The platform visibly turns in both directions as our output. This gives us a clean, testable proof-of-concept that we can improve later with an H-bridge and stronger mechanical coupling to a wheel.

What we learned

Prototype small first

Scaling down made progress visible quickly and reduced risk. We could test the idea without building the whole simulator.

Hardware matters

If the goal is bidirectional DC motor motion, you need an H-bridge (or similar driver). Knowing this earlier would’ve saved time.

Iteration beats perfection

Documenting failures helped us converge: each “no” narrowed the path to a working demo.

Next iteration plan

Add an H-bridge, tune input mapping for smooth turning, and connect the motor output to an actual steering wheel or gear system to increase torque.
