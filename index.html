Semester Engineering Build

From big ideas to a smaller, working force-feedback prototype.

We set out to build a driving simulator. Early on, we explored big-scale approaches like linear actuators. As we learned more, we scaled down to a simpler but testable system: a steering wheel mechanism driven by a motor that can rotate both directions. Our final demo mounts a toy model car to a rotating motor platform.

What we built

A compact proof-of-concept for steering feedback and control. Instead of a full rig, we focused on getting clean, repeatable motion first—then documenting what worked and what didn’t.

Goal

Create a driving simulator experience by translating joystick inputs into steering motion on a physical wheel or platform.

Shift in approach

We explored linear actuators and other big mechanisms, but moved to a smaller rotating motor design to get faster iteration.

Final demo

A toy model car mounted on a rotating motor platform that turns clockwise and counterclockwise as a controllable steering output.

Why it mattered

Engineering tradeoffs

We learned that “bigger” isn’t always better. Smaller prototypes can prove the core concept sooner and with fewer unknowns.

Motor control reality

Reversing a DC motor reliably requires proper direction control hardware (like an H-bridge), not just code changes.

Documentation

Recording what failed helped us converge faster and made the final outcome easier to explain and defend.
